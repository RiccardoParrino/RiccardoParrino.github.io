Introduction to Information Management Systems

#information #management #systems #development #tailormade #ERP

Introduction

In this short post, we aim to explore the field of business informatics — the discipline that studies the application of information technology to companies and the impact of IT on the various components that make up the business system. In just a few lines, we will provide an overview of the different areas within a information management system and how they can work together to improve overall efficiency. Additionally, we'll take a brief look at emerging technologies and potential future developments in the field.

Prerequisites

For this short guide on ERP and management software, no prior knowledge or prerequisites are required — neither in terms of programming nor in terms of organizational or economic background.

Content

ERPs are systems that originated and developed primarily within the manufacturing industry. They are designed to meet the practical needs of businesses, such as transaction recording, planning and control, knowledge acquisition and organization, as well as the management and coordination of business production processes.

An ERP system is essentially structured around the administrative area, logistics, sales or commercial area, purchasing, production, and additionally includes some complementary systems.

The administrative area is responsible for managing the company's accounting, financial, and VAT transactions. The main data it works with includes: the chart of accounts, the list of customers, the list of suppliers, and the financial institutions the company works with.

The logistics area is responsible for tracking all material movements within the warehouse. It works with item master data, the company layout (i.e., the logical organization of the warehouse), and must keep track of what is being moved, when it is moved, where it is moved, in what quantity, how it is moved, and why.

The sales area and the purchasing area are symmetrical in many respects.
The sales or commercial area of a company is responsible for managing sales and customer relations. It primarily works with the commercial master data of customers and sales products, as well as with data structures that define price lists, discount schemes, and agency agreements. This area mainly handles process plans, such as shipping and invoicing schedules.

The purchasing area is responsible for tracking all purchases made on behalf of the company. The main data it works with includes purchase products, material and service suppliers, the company's price lists, and discount lists. The purchasing department also manages purchase orders along with the related process documents and invoices.

A production management system is responsible for overseeing the company's manufacturing processes. The key data it operates on includes the master data structures that define a product's components and the related operations (the so-called bill of materials and work orders), the estimated and actual production costs, and the data structures that encode the production process along with the related material and labor requirements.

Additional systems may be necessary for better management of the company. These can include systems dedicated to product quality control, personnel management, or even tracking research and innovation activities within the company. New systems can integrate emerging technologies such as IoT (Internet of Things) for more precise monitoring of processes or machinery health, or cost-reduction strategies through the use of Cloud technologies, as opposed to traditional on-premise computing solutions. Mobile technologies and related apps could also be employed for constant monitoring and updating of the company's status. Among the most widespread and pervasive technologies today, artificial intelligence certainly stands out, finding broad applications in this field—for example, in automating and optimizing production processes.

Conclusion

As mentioned in the introduction, we have explored the field of business informatics, providing a general overview of the various areas that make up an ERP system. At the end of the guide, we also offered a brief overview of the areas of innovation and progress that can be applied in this field.
